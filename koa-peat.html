The Headline

A warrior named on purpose

In Hawaiian, “Koa” means strong warrior. Koa Peat has spent his entire life making the name look like a scouting report. He is the youngest of seven children in one of Arizona’s great athletic families, a four-time state champion who never lost a title game, and the only player in USA Basketball history to win four men’s junior gold medals. Now, after a freshman season that carried Arizona to its first Final Four in a quarter-century, the 19-year-old from Gilbert is coming home to play for the Suns.

For a franchise rebuilding its identity around youth, toughness, and a “locked-in” culture, Peat is not a project. He is a proven winner who has answered every question the game has asked him — usually by raising the trophy at the end.

He plays like the stakes are personal. Wherever Koa Peat has been, the scoreboard at the end has read the same way. AVC Scouting Note

The Bloodline

Built by a family of pros

Toughness is the family business. Koa’s father, Todd Peat, played nine seasons as an NFL offensive guard for the Cardinals and Raiders. His brother Andrus Peat became a first-round NFL offensive lineman. Across seven siblings, nearly every Peat went on to play college or professional sports — football and basketball both. Koa grew up the youngest, competing against bigger, stronger, older athletes every single day. It shows in how he plays.

The Scouting Report

How he wins — and where he grows

Peat projects as a powerful, versatile combo forward: too quick for bigger bigs, too strong for smaller wings. The frame is NBA-ready today; the jumper is the swing skill for his ceiling.

Strengths

  • Elite rim finisher — physical through contact, 67% at the basket
  • NBA-ready body — 245 lbs with a 6'11"+ wingspan and real motor
  • Connective passer — high IQ, 2.6 assists to just 1.6 turnovers
  • Two-way versatility — can guard bigs and switch onto the perimeter
  • Battle-tested — thrives on the biggest stages, on every level

Areas to Develop

  • Three-point range — mechanics and volume need to become reliable
  • Free-throw touch — 62% leaves points on the table
  • Handle in space — limited advanced separation off the dribble
  • Less power-reliant — adding finesse vs. NBA-sized bigs
The Fit in Phoenix

Why he’s a Sun

The Suns have moved past the superteam era into a younger, cost-controlled, identity-driven build around Devin Booker. Head coach Jordan Ott is forging a culture defined by resilience and a “locked-in” mindset — players who are bothered when they lose. Peat is a near-perfect cultural fit: a winner with a blue-collar motor, drafted onto a rookie contract that protects the franchise’s long-term flexibility.

And then there’s the story. A Gilbert kid — raised 20 minutes from the arena, a state-title fixture Valley fans have watched grow up — now wearing the Suns. That’s not just a roster move. It’s a marketing and community gift.

Found money with a hometown jersey. High-upside, cost-controlled, and already beloved in the Valley. The front-office case
The Person

Off the floor

Peat’s reputation off the court matches the on-court résumé. In high school he volunteered with the Gila River Indian Community and coached youth basketball and football — giving back to the same Arizona community that raised him. Teammates and coaches describe a player who leads by standard, not by speech: he sets the tone with how hard he plays and expects everyone to meet it.

The broken-hand story is the one that travels. Two days before the state quarterfinals of his senior year, Peat broke his right hand. He had surgery, skipped the McDonald’s All-American Game and Nike Hoop Summit to protect his future — and still came back to lead Perry to a fourth straight championship. That’s the competitor the Suns just drafted.

For the Front Office

Conversation starters · when you call Koa
  1. Welcome him home. He grew up in Gilbert and starred at Perry. Tell him what it means to the Valley that he’s a Sun — this is personal for him, and for the fan base.
  2. Honor the bloodline. Ask about growing up the youngest of seven in the Peat athletic family — his dad’s and brother’s NFL careers shaped his toughness. It’s the fastest way to his story.
  3. Respect the winning. Four state titles, four USA Basketball golds, a Final Four. Let him know you see him as a winner first — that’s the identity he carries.
  4. Name the warrior. “Koa” means strong warrior. It’s a meaningful nod to his Hawaiian heritage and exactly the player you drafted.
  5. Paint the development picture. He knows the jumper is the next step. Show him the plan — player development, the role next to Booker, the path to minutes under Jordan Ott.
  6. Invoke the comeback. Reference the broken hand and the fourth title. It tells him you did the homework and you value who he is, not just the stat line.
